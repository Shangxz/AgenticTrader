**One-paragraph thesis.** Everest is a scaled, well-capitalized global reinsurer/insurer trading *below* book value (~0.89x) and at ~7x trailing earnings — a clear statistical discount to P&C peers (ACGL 1.43x book, AXS 1.33x, RNR 1.23x). The discount exists for a concrete reason: in 2024 Everest took **$1.7B of US-casualty reserve strengthening** on social-inflation-driven prior-year development, culminating in a Q4 2024 net loss of **-$13.96/share**, the departure of its CEO, and a credibility hit. Under new CEO Jim Williamson, the company bought a **$1.2B adverse-development cover**, refocused on profitability over volume, and printed a **91.2% combined ratio with $16.21 EPS in Q1 2026**. The mispricing question is whether the reserve problem is now ring-fenced (in which case a sub-book, double-digit-ROE reinsurer is too cheap) or whether further casualty creep keeps the discount permanent.

## PROMPT 1 — Buy-Side Mispricing Analysis

*Acting as an experienced buy-side analyst specializing in mispriced/undervalued companies.*

**Company:** Everest Group (EG)
**Current valuation context:** ~0.89x book and ~7x trailing earnings vs P&C-(re)insurance peers at ~1.2–1.4x book and 5–8x earnings.

**1. Business-model clarity — is this genuinely hard to model?**
Everest is not conceptually complex — it underwrites reinsurance (~65% of premium) and primary insurance (~35%), and earns an investment-income spread on float. What makes it *hard to trust* is **reserve adequacy in long-tail US casualty**. Casualty losses (general liability, commercial auto, professional lines) develop over many years; an insurer books a best-estimate reserve today and the truth emerges over a decade. "Social inflation" — rising jury verdicts, litigation funding, broader liability theories — has repeatedly forced the whole industry to top up old reserves. So the analyst's difficulty is not the business; it is **the believability of one number on the balance sheet** (loss reserves) that, if wrong, flows straight through book value. That single point of opacity is exactly why the market applies a below-book multiple.

**2. Earnings quality — fair reflection of economic reality?**
Q1 2026 earnings look *high-quality and arguably understated*: the 91.2% combined ratio was helped by only $130M of catastrophe losses (vs $472M a year earlier) but was *also* supported by an **88.5% attritional combined ratio** (i.e., excluding cats), which is the cleaner read on underlying underwriting — that is genuinely good. Prior-year reserve development was a modest **favorable $33M**, not a release-driven flatter. Investment income is now a large, durable earnings engine (record ~$2B in 2024 and rising as the portfolio reinvests at higher yields). The honest caveat: a single benign-cat quarter is not a trend, and the *trailing* P/E of 7x sits on earnings still partly depressed by 2024–25 reserve noise — normalized earnings power (mid-teens operating ROE on ~$383 book ≈ $55–65 EPS) implies an even lower ~5–6x forward multiple if the reserves hold.

**3. Hidden assets / optionality.**
- **Below-book trading itself is the optionality**: every dollar of buyback below book value is *accretive to book value per share*. Everest has a **$4.74B repurchase authorization** with a $300M/quarter floor and bought back $331M in Q1 2026; management flagged ~$1B of buybacks likely in 2026, with book value expected to cross **$400/share**. ([Simply Wall St](https://simplywall.st/stocks/us/insurance/nyse-eg/everest-group/news/will-everests-reinsurance-pivot-and-us474-billion-buyback-ch/amp))
- **The $1.2B adverse-development cover (ADC)** purchased in Oct 2025 caps a chunk of the legacy casualty tail — third-party validation that reduces left-tail reserve risk. ([Carrier Management](https://www.carriermanagement.com/news/2025/10/28/280845.htm))
- A large, high-quality fixed-income portfolio re-investing at elevated yields gives a multi-year tailwind to net investment income that consensus tends to under-model.

**4. Recent sentiment — temporary or structural repricing?**
The de-rating was event-driven: the stock fell ~18% and ~13% on a single day around the July 2024 and January 2025 reserve announcements, plus a CEO change. ([Reinsurance News](https://www.reinsurancene.ws/everest-reports-total-reserve-strengthening-of-1-7bn-for-2024/)) That is the signature of a **confidence shock**, not a demonstrated collapse in earning power — the franchise, ratings, and capital base remained intact, and Q1 2026 (+210% net income y/y, 91.2% CR) is the first clear data point that the "new Everest" underwrites profitably. The market is repricing slowly because it wants *several* clean reserve quarters before extending trust.

**5. Who is selling, and why?**
- **Burned momentum / growth holders** who owned EG as a "compounding book value" story exited when book-value-per-share growth stalled and the reserve credibility broke.
- **Insurance-dedicated funds** rotated toward peers with cleaner reserve track records (ACGL, RNR) — a relative-value trade that mechanically pressures EG regardless of absolute value.
- **Tax-loss and index-rebalance flows** after the 2024–25 drawdown. None of these sellers are necessarily acting on a *fundamental* re-underwriting of the business — they are reacting to a reserve headline and a leadership change, which is the classic setup for a forced/incentivized seller leaving value behind.

**What the market must believe to justify ~0.89x book — and is it reasonable?**
The sub-book price implies the market believes **Everest's stated reserves are still inadequate** — i.e., that more casualty charges are coming that will erode book value, *and/or* that normalized ROE will sit below cost of equity (low-double-digits). For that to be the right call, 2024's $1.7B strengthening plus the 2025 ADC must have *failed* to clean the book. That is possible — casualty reserving is genuinely uncertain — but the weight of evidence (ADC purchase, conservative new-CEO posture, modest *favorable* Q1 development, 88.5% attritional CR) argues the reserves are now adequate-to-conservative. If so, a scaled reinsurer earning mid-teens ROE should trade at/above book, not below it. **The required belief (further material reserve deterioration) is plausible but, on current evidence, looks too pessimistic — which is the mispricing.**

## PROMPT 2 — Comparable Company Analysis

*Acting as a valuation analyst building a comp set for an investment committee.*

**Company:** Everest Group (EG)
**Current multiple:** **0.89x book value** (and ~7x trailing P/E)
**Sector:** Global P&C reinsurance + specialty insurance

**Step 1 — The right peer group.** The cleanest comps are Bermuda/global reinsurers and specialty (re)insurers with a similar property-cat + casualty + specialty mix:

| Peer | P/B | Trailing P/E | Why it belongs | Source (Robinhood, 2026-06-25) |

| **Arch Capital (ACGL)** | 1.43x | 7.3x | Bermuda specialty insurance + reinsurance + mortgage; best-in-class underwriter — the "quality" benchmark | Robinhood |
| **RenaissanceRe (RNR)** | 1.23x | 5.1x | Pure-play global reinsurer, heavy property-cat — closest reinsurance-mix comp | Robinhood |
| **AXIS Capital (AXS)** | 1.33x | 7.7x | Bermuda specialty insurance + reinsurance, similar scale tier | Robinhood |
| **Arch / RNR / AXS average** | **~1.33x** | **~6.7x** | — | — |

*Poor comps to avoid:* primary US personal-lines carriers (Progressive, Allstate) — different risk, no reinsurance mix, and momentum-priced; pure life/health insurers; and Lloyd's-only specialists. They look superficially similar ("insurance") but trade on different drivers.

**Step 2 — Quantify the gap.** On the metric that matters most for an insurer, **price/book**:
- Peer-group average P/B ≈ **1.33x**; Everest ≈ **0.89x**.
- Everest trades at roughly a **33% discount to the peer average on book value** — and is the *only* name in the group below 1.0x.
- On trailing P/E the gap is smaller (~7x EG vs ~6.7x peer avg), i.e., **the discount is almost entirely a price/book story**, which is exactly what a reserve-credibility discount looks like (the market is marking down the *quality/reliability of book*, not current-year earnings).

**Step 3 — Explain the gap.** Decomposing the ~33% book discount:
- **Reserve credibility (the dominant factor, ~20–25 pts):** the $1.7B 2024 casualty charge and prior 2021/2023 top-ups ($400M/$392M) gave Everest the *worst recent reserve track record* in the peer set. ACGL and RNR have not had charges of that magnitude — hence their book is "trusted" at 1.2–1.4x.
- **ROE / mix (~5–8 pts):** Everest's reinsurance-heavy book is more cat-exposed and lower-ROE-stable than Arch's diversified machine (Arch's mortgage segment earns very high returns), justifying *some* discount to ACGL specifically.
- **Leadership/strategy uncertainty (~3–5 pts):** new CEO, "profitability over volume" pivot, and shrinking top line create execution uncertainty the market hasn't yet rewarded.
- *Not* explained by leverage or capital weakness — Everest is well-capitalized and actively buying back stock, so this is not a balance-sheet-distress discount.

**Step 4 — What would close the gap?** To re-rate from 0.89x toward the **peer median ~1.3x book**:
1. **Two-to-three more quarters of flat-to-favorable casualty reserve development** — the single biggest lever; it directly rebuilds the "trust" the 2024 charge destroyed. Q1 2026's +$33M favorable PYD is the first installment.
2. **Sustained sub-92% attritional combined ratios**, proving underwriting quality independent of cat luck (Q1's 88.5% is a strong start).
3. **Book-value-per-share growth back to double digits** (management guides BV > $400) plus continued below-book buybacks, which compound per-share book.
4. **No new shock** — simply *not* announcing another casualty charge through 2026 would itself drive a meaningful re-rating.

How achievable? The early trajectory (one clean quarter, ADC in place, disciplined capital return) is encouraging, but reserve trust is rebuilt slowly. A re-rate to even **1.1–1.2x book on a ~$390–400 book** implies a **~$430–480 price (≈ 25–40% upside)**; merely closing to 1.0x book is ~$390 (~14%). The risk/reward is asymmetric *if* the reserves hold.

## PROMPT 3 — Catalyst & Value-Trap Analysis

*Acting as a value-oriented PM deciding whether EG earns a slot in a concentrated book.*

**Company:** Everest Group (EG)
**Thesis:** Everest is a scaled, well-capitalized global reinsurer trading below book and ~7x earnings because a 2024 US-casualty reserve charge broke market confidence. A new CEO, a $1.2B adverse-development cover, profitability-over-volume underwriting, a 91.2% Q1'26 combined ratio, and a $4.74B buyback (accretive below book) suggest the reserve problem is being ring-fenced and the franchise can re-earn a 1.2x+ book multiple.
**Approx. discount to my intrinsic-value estimate (~$430–470, i.e., ~1.1–1.2x normalized book):** **~20–30%.**

**Catalyst analysis**

1. **Specific re-rating events / milestones:**
   - **Clean reserve quarters** (Q2/Q3/Q4 2026) with flat-to-favorable prior-year development — the core trust-rebuild.
   - **Book value crossing $400/share** (management-guided) plus visible per-share accretion from below-book buybacks.
   - **Continued sub-92% attritional combined ratios** demonstrating underwriting profitability through the softening reinsurance cycle.
   - **Capital-return escalation** (buyback above the $300M/quarter floor, or a dividend increase) signaling management's own confidence in reserve adequacy and excess capital.
2. **Likelihood / timeframe:** Clean reserve development quarter-to-quarter is the *base case* now that the ADC is in place and 2024 actions were large — **moderately high probability over 12–24 months**, but punctuated by event risk every quarterly disclosure. Book > $400 is **high probability within 12 months** given current earnings + buyback math.
3. **Biggest-impact catalyst:** **A full year (2026) with no new casualty charge.** That single absence would do more to re-rate the stock than any positive announcement, because the entire discount is a reserve-distrust discount.
4. **Near-term catalysts that could work *against* the thesis even if the long-term view is right:**
   - The **reinsurance pricing cycle is softening** — property-cat rates fell **~14.7% at the 1/1/2026 renewal** (biggest drop since 2014), and property rates down 10–20%. ([Artemis](https://www.artemis.bm/news/january-reinsurance-renewal-accelerated-softening-drives-double-digit-declines-guy-carpenter/)) That pressures top line and future margins, and Everest is deliberately *shrinking* into it — optically weak premium growth could spook momentum investors.
   - A heavy **2026 Atlantic hurricane / cat season** would hit a cat-exposed reinsurer hard and reset the combined ratio, masking the underlying improvement for a quarter or two.

**Value-trap check**

5. **Three common reasons stocks like this never re-rate, and which apply:**
   - **(a) The reserve charge was the first of several, not the last** — *this is the live risk.* Social inflation has repeatedly forced *serial* casualty top-ups across the industry (Everest itself charged in 2021, 2023, and 2024). If 2024 + the ADC did not fully clean the book, the sub-book multiple is *correct*, and the stock is a value trap. This is the single most important thing to monitor.
   - **(b) Soft-market margin compression** — re-rating requires improving returns, but a softening reinsurance cycle can erode ROE faster than reserves heal, capping the multiple. Partially applies.
   - **(c) Permanent "show-me" discount** — some serial-charge insurers carry a structural discount for years even after fixing the book (the market demands a long, unblemished record). Applies to a degree; the re-rate may be slower than the fundamentals justify.
6. **Management / governance / capital-allocation issues:** This is actually a *relative strength*. The new CEO inherited the problem rather than caused it, took a large kitchen-sink reserve action plus an ADC (the credible playbook), and is returning capital aggressively via below-book buybacks — the most value-accretive use of cash at this price. There is no controlling shareholder, no dual-class structure, and capital allocation is shareholder-friendly. The governance risk is mainly *execution* (will the new team underwrite as disciplined as it promises through a soft market), not misalignment.

**Risk/reward verdict.** The discount is real and the catalyst (the *absence* of further casualty charges) is concrete, measurable each quarter, and supported by the ADC and conservative new-management posture. You are paid to wait via a ~2.3% dividend plus per-share book accretion from below-book buybacks, with downside cushioned by trading *below* tangible book. The dominant risk — further reserve deterioration — is genuine and not fully knowable from outside, so this is a **medium-sized, monitor-the-reserves position, not a max-conviction one.** At ~0.89x book with mid-teens normalized ROE and a credible clean-up underway, the risk/reward of waiting 12–24 months for the re-rate is attractive *provided* each quarterly reserve disclosure stays clean — and the thesis should be cut quickly if a new casualty charge appears.
