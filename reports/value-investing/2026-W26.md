## 2026-06-26 Fresh Market Context

The U.S. equity market still looks expensive in aggregate, so the bar for calling any stock undervalued is high. StockAnalysis' U.S. screener showed 5,591 listed U.S. stocks during this run, with mega-cap leadership still concentrated in large technology and AI-linked names. A current MarketWatch/LSEG screen put the S&P 500 forward P/E around 20.3, down from 22.5 at the end of 2025 but still not broadly cheap. Market tone on June 26 was mixed-to-defensive: the S&P 500 and Nasdaq were under pressure from a technology selloff while value-oriented areas such as healthcare and financials looked comparatively more useful for quality-value hunting.

The screen therefore favored companies with durable cash generation, strong market positions, manageable balance-sheet risk, and valuation support from earnings, free cash flow, book value, or depressed-but-recoverable cycle expectations. Purely low-multiple names with weak or structurally impaired business quality were excluded or placed on watchlist.

## Today's Top Ideas

### 1. Thermo Fisher Scientific (TMO)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** TMO traded around $506 during the run with a trailing P/E near 28. That is not statistically cheap, but the price still reflects a subdued life-science tools cycle rather than Thermo Fisher's normalized earnings power. Q1 adjusted EPS of $5.44 annualizes to a much lower effective multiple than the GAAP trailing P/E implies, and the stock remains meaningfully below prior cycle expectations for a scaled life-science leader.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Q1 revenue grew 6% to $11.01 billion, adjusted EPS grew 6%, adjusted operating margin was 21.8%, and management continued to repurchase stock and raise the dividend. Thermo Fisher has scale, recurring consumables and services exposure, sticky customer workflows, and a long record of disciplined capital allocation.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** Organic revenue growth was only 1%, and biopharma funding, academic research budgets, and acquisition-related debt still matter. If the life-science tools cycle remains slow, the valuation gap may take time to close.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $506; trailing P/E around 28; Q1 adjusted EPS $5.44; high-margin recurring/scaled franchise.

### 2. Arch Capital Group (ACGL)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** ACGL traded around $94 with a trailing P/E near 7.3, giving it one of the clearest statistical value signals among high-quality financials reviewed today.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Arch remains a strong specialty insurance and reinsurance underwriter. Q1 results showed strong underwriting profitability, with combined ratio of 91.2%, underwriting income of $316 million, and continued capital return. Book value per share was $383.75, or $393.02 excluding unrealized fixed-income marks.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** Insurance earnings are cyclical and can be flattered by benign catastrophe periods, reserve releases, and investment income. Catastrophe losses, reserve adequacy, pricing, and underwriting discipline matter more than the headline P/E.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $94; trailing P/E around 7; strong combined ratio; price below reported book value, though book-value comparisons require care because ACGL's quote and book value are per common share under its own reporting base.

### 3. Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** BRK.B traded around $488 with a trailing P/E near 14.5. For a diversified collection of insurance, railroad, energy, manufacturing, service, retail, and investment assets, that is a reasonable multiple, especially with the balance sheet holding very large cash and Treasury reserves.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Berkshire's Q1 operating earnings rose materially year over year, and outside summaries of the filing noted operating earnings of about $11.3 billion and record cash/Treasury holdings near $380 billion. The company retains exceptional liquidity, underwriting depth, tax-efficient float, and optionality to buy assets in market dislocations.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** The cash pile can dilute returns if not deployed well, post-Buffett capital allocation needs to prove itself over time, and a conglomerate discount can persist. Berkshire is also less likely to be dramatically mispriced than smaller companies.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $488; trailing P/E around 14.5; roughly 1.4x book value cited in current public summaries; unusually strong liquidity.

### 4. Medtronic (MDT)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** MDT traded around $81. FY2026 non-GAAP EPS was $5.53, implying roughly a mid-teens adjusted earnings multiple for a global medical-device franchise.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** FY2026 revenue grew 8.4% reported and 5.8% organic to $36.4 billion, the strongest annual top-line growth Medtronic has delivered in 10 years. Free cash flow was $5.4 billion, and the company raised the dividend for the 49th consecutive year.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** Non-GAAP operating margin declined, tariff and product-cycle pressure remain relevant, and Medtronic has had prior execution disappointments. The diabetes separation, robotics progress, and cardiac-ablation competition should be monitored.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $81; FY2026 adjusted EPS $5.53; adjusted earnings multiple around 14-15x; dividend support.

### 5. Alphabet (GOOGL)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** GOOGL traded around $344 with a trailing P/E near 26. This is not low-multiple value, but the valuation remains reasonable relative to Alphabet's growth, margins, balance sheet, and AI/search/cloud optionality.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Q1 revenue increased 22% to $109.9 billion, operating income increased 30%, and operating margin expanded to 36.1%. Alphabet remains one of the highest-quality cash-generating franchises in public equities.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** AI could pressure search behavior or monetization, capital spending is high, and antitrust risk remains material. Q1 net income was boosted by unrealized investment gains, so operating income and free cash flow deserve more weight than EPS growth.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $344; trailing P/E around 26; Q1 operating margin above 36%; large net cash and marketable securities base.

### 6. Danaher (DHR)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** DHR traded around $193 with a trailing P/E near 37, so it is not a statistical bargain. The value case rests on buying a high-quality compounder while core bioprocessing and diagnostics demand remains below normal.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Q1 adjusted EPS grew 9.5% to $2.06, revenue rose 3.5% to $6.0 billion, operating cash flow was $1.3 billion, and non-GAAP free cash flow was $1.1 billion. Cash conversion and the Danaher Business System remain strong quality signals.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** Core revenue growth was only 0.5%, diagnostics can remain weak, and recent acquisition activity adds execution risk. DHR ranks below TMO today because the headline valuation leaves less room for error.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $193; trailing P/E around 37; Q1 FCF of $1.1 billion; raised adjusted EPS guidance after Q1.

### 7. PayPal (PYPL)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** PYPL traded around $42 with a trailing P/E near 8, making it one of the cheapest profitable large-cap technology/payment platforms in the scan.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Q1 total payment volume increased 11% to $464 billion, payment transactions increased 7%, free cash flow was $0.9 billion, adjusted free cash flow was $1.7 billion, and the company returned $1.5 billion through buybacks in the quarter. PayPal still has a large two-sided network and meaningful cash generation.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** This is a lower-confidence quality idea. GAAP operating margin contracted, non-GAAP operating income fell, active-account growth was only 1%, and competition from cards, wallets, Apple Pay, and merchant processors remains intense. Cheapness could be a value trap if margins and engagement keep deteriorating.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $42; trailing P/E around 8; Q1 adjusted free cash flow $1.7 billion; margin pressure keeps the ranking lower.

### 8. Elevance Health (ELV)

- **Why it looks undervalued:** ELV traded around $387 with a trailing P/E near 16. Management's previously raised 2026 adjusted EPS guidance implies a lower forward multiple if achieved.
- **Why it still qualifies as quality:** Elevance is a scaled health benefits and services company, and its Q1 report pointed to operating performance supported by actions taken in prior quarters. Scale, data, provider relationships, and diversified benefits/services exposure remain meaningful quality traits.
- **Risks / disconfirming factors:** This carries the heaviest fundamental risk among today's top ideas. Medicaid and Medicare Advantage margins, utilization, medical-cost trend, CMS policy, and regulatory scrutiny can overwhelm headline cheapness. Confidence remains moderate to low until medical-cost stability is clearer.
- **Valuation signals:** Current quote around $387; trailing P/E around 16; apparent forward earnings discount offset by above-average policy and medical-cost risk.

## Watchlist / Not Selected

- **Qualcomm (QCOM):** The strategic case improved after management raised its fiscal 2029 non-handset revenue target to $40 billion, including more than $15 billion from data centers, and disclosed major data-center ambitions. However, the stock traded near $205 after a sharp investor-day rally, with a trailing P/E around 22. The opportunity is credible but now depends heavily on multi-year execution in data-center AI, automotive, IoT, and software against entrenched competitors.
- **Everest Group (EG):** External screens flag EG as optically cheap on P/E, P/B, and leverage, and Q1 underwriting results were solid. It was not selected over ACGL because current quote services showed a less clean trailing P/E snapshot during today's run and because ACGL's underwriting record and valuation signal looked stronger today.
- **Global Payments (GPN):** NerdWallet/Finviz screens show low P/B and low debt/equity, but recent quote data showed negative trailing EPS. That makes it less suitable for a quality-value top list without a deeper turnaround review.
- **Nike (NKE), Estee Lauder (EL), and other wide-moat recovery names:** These can be undervalued if brand recovery materializes, but current earnings quality and visibility were weaker than the selected names.
- **Micron and AI hardware momentum names:** Earnings momentum is strong, but recent price moves and cyclicality make them less clean as quality-value candidates today.

## Conclusion

The strongest current quality-value candidates are **Thermo Fisher Scientific (TMO)**, **Arch Capital (ACGL)**, and **Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B)**. TMO offers high-quality life-science exposure at a depressed-cycle price; ACGL offers the clearest statistical value signal with strong underwriting quality; Berkshire offers a moderate multiple, fortress liquidity, and downturn optionality. **Medtronic (MDT)** and **Alphabet (GOOGL)** remain attractive quality-at-a-reasonable-price candidates. **Danaher (DHR)** is high quality but less cheap than TMO. **PayPal (PYPL)** is the most statistically cheap technology/payment idea, but its margin and competitive risks keep confidence lower. **Elevance (ELV)** remains inexpensive but riskier because healthcare-policy and medical-cost uncertainty are unusually important.
