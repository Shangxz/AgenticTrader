# Value Investing Report - ISO Week 2026-29

Latest run date: 2026-07-14

This report is research support only, not personal financial advice. Each update starts from a fresh quality-value review of US-listed public equities. Earlier entries from the same week are preserved as a record, but they are not used to steer the current ranking.

## Market Context

The US market still looks selectively priced rather than broadly cheap. FactSet's July 10, 2026 Earnings Insight puts the S&P 500 forward 12-month P/E at 20.5, above both the 5-year average of 19.9 and the 10-year average of 19.0. FactSet also shows a trailing 12-month P/E of 28.0, with Energy and Financials the lowest-multiple sectors and Consumer Discretionary and Industrials the highest-multiple sectors.

Second-quarter earnings season is beginning with unusually heavy bank reporting on July 14, 2026. Recent market commentary points to better bank earnings from trading, investment banking, and loan growth, but also flags valuation and macro risks from high expectations, geopolitical tension, inflation pressure, and AI-capex concentration. This remains an environment where the quality-value bar should be fairly strict: cheap multiples alone are not enough.

## Current Fresh Ranking - 2026-07-14

The July 14 screen emphasized low or reasonable earnings/free-cash-flow/book-value multiples, returns on equity or invested capital, balance-sheet risk, cash conversion, shareholder yield, and business durability. The strongest current ideas are still concentrated in specialty insurance, healthcare services/pharma, and select consumer/technology cash-flow compounders.

### 1. Arch Capital Group (ACGL)

**Why it looks undervalued:** ACGL remains the cleanest quality-value blend in the July 14 screen. StockAnalysis shows a trailing P/E of 7.90, forward P/E of 11.05, P/FCF of 6.12, and EV/FCF of 6.43. That is a low cash-flow valuation for a profitable specialty insurer with strong recent underwriting profitability.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** The quality profile is unusually strong for the valuation: ROE of 21.31%, ROIC of 18.41%, operating margin of 28.94%, profit margin of 24.63%, and FCF margin of 29.63%. Debt/equity is only 0.11, debt/FCF is 0.47, and interest coverage is 38.15x. Share count is down 3.09% year over year.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Insurance earnings are cyclical and catastrophe-sensitive. The low multiple may partly reflect concern that underwriting margins and investment income are above normalized levels. Analyst growth forecasts are modest, so ACGL should not be treated as a high-growth compounder at any price.

**Notable valuation signals:** Earnings yield is 13.47%, FCF yield is 16.34%, and buyback yield is 3.09%. The current setup still offers the best combination of valuation, quality, and balance-sheet strength in today's screen.

### 2. The Cigna Group (CI)

**Why it looks undervalued:** CI trades at a modest valuation for a large, cash-generative healthcare franchise: trailing P/E of 12.90, forward P/E of 9.86, P/FCF of 10.52, EV/EBITDA of 7.74, and EV/FCF of 13.52. Its PEG ratio is listed at 0.97, which is attractive if consensus growth is even roughly directionally right.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** CI has ROE of 16.27% and ROIC of 14.34%, with debt/EBITDA of 2.31 and interest coverage of 8.33x. It generated $7.66 billion of free cash flow over the last 12 months. Share count is down 4.83% year over year, and the company combines a 2.05% dividend yield with a 4.83% buyback yield.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Healthcare insurers and pharmacy benefit managers face persistent regulatory, reimbursement, medical-cost, and political risk. Margins are structurally thin, so policy changes or cost surprises can move earnings quickly.

**Notable valuation signals:** FCF yield is 9.51%, earnings yield is 7.81%, and total shareholder yield is 6.95%. CI moves ahead of Everest in today's ranking because its cash-flow data is clearer and its earnings-growth outlook is more balanced.

### 3. Everest Group (EG)

**Why it looks undervalued:** EG remains compelling on insurance valuation metrics: trailing P/E of 7.70, forward P/E of 7.15, PEG ratio of 0.58, and P/B of 0.98. The stock is still priced near book value despite double-digit returns on equity.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** Everest shows ROE of 13.82%, ROIC of 11.50%, operating margin of 14.76%, and debt/equity of 0.23. Interest coverage is 17.17x, and share count is down 3.70% year over year. The 2.12% dividend yield plus 3.70% buyback yield produces a shareholder yield of 5.86%.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** As with ACGL, underwriting cyclicality and catastrophe losses can distort normalized profitability. Revenue-growth forecasts are negative, so the thesis depends on underwriting discipline, capital returns, and valuation support rather than strong top-line expansion.

**Notable valuation signals:** The earnings yield is 13.44%, and the stock trades just below book value. I rank it slightly below ACGL and CI today because free-cash-flow figures are less clean for the insurance model and the revenue outlook is weaker.

### 4. Constellation Brands (STZ)

**Why it looks undervalued:** STZ trades at 12.84x trailing earnings, 11.36x forward earnings, and 12.52x P/FCF after a 21.88% 52-week decline. The valuation is not distressed, but it is low for a branded beer franchise if cash conversion and brand share remain durable.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** The company has strong brand economics and margins: ROE of 23.69%, ROIC of 11.93%, operating margin of 33.60%, profit margin of 20.14%, and FCF margin of 20.25%. Recent commentary on the June quarter noted that FCF rose despite softer sales, suggesting management is extracting cash even in a slower growth patch.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Debt is meaningful: debt/equity is 1.23, debt/FCF is 5.74, and net debt is above $10 billion. Growth forecasts are modest, and beer-category pressure could make the current multiple a fair discount rather than a bargain.

**Notable valuation signals:** FCF yield is 7.99%, earnings yield is 7.94%, dividend yield is 3.06%, buyback yield is 3.36%, and shareholder yield is 6.43%. STZ is a qualified value idea, but with more leverage and category-growth caveats than ACGL or CI.

### 5. Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY)

**Why it looks undervalued:** BMY trades at 16.61x trailing earnings, 9.70x forward earnings, 10.18x P/FCF, 8.26x EV/EBITDA, and 13.16x EV/FCF. The market is still assigning a low forward multiple to a large cash-flow base.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** BMY generated $11.91 billion of free cash flow over the last 12 months, with ROE of 38.73%, ROIC of 20.88%, gross margin of 71.98%, operating margin of 31.56%, and FCF margin of 24.56%. The Piotroski F-score is 7, which supports the idea that the business is not merely optically cheap.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Patent cliffs, pipeline execution, acquisition integration, and leverage remain central risks. Debt/equity is 2.31 and debt/FCF is 3.90. Analyst consensus is only Hold, with negative 3-year revenue and EPS growth forecasts. This is a cash-flow value idea, not a clean growth-quality idea.

**Notable valuation signals:** FCF yield is 9.83%, dividend yield is 4.25%, and forward P/E is below 10x. BMY remains on the top list, but confidence is lower than for ACGL, CI, and EG because the disconfirming factors are more fundamental.

### 6. QUALCOMM (QCOM)

**Why it looks undervalued:** QCOM is not as statistically cheap as the insurers or healthcare names, but it screens as a higher-quality value candidate after recent weakness. It trades at 20.10x trailing earnings, 18.70x forward earnings, 15.51x P/FCF, and 15.95x EV/FCF, with the stock below its 50-day moving average and still reasonably valued relative to its returns on capital.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** QCOM has ROE of 36.08%, ROIC of 32.94%, FCF margin of 28.10%, operating margin of 25.68%, and an Altman Z-score of 5.76. Debt/FCF is only 1.22 and interest coverage is 16.82x. The company generated $12.50 billion of free cash flow over the last 12 months and has reduced share count by 3.65% year over year.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** The valuation is only moderately cheap, not deeply cheap. Revenue and EPS growth forecasts are modest, analyst consensus is Hold, and the business remains exposed to handset cycles, customer concentration, China/geopolitical risk, and semiconductor competition.

**Notable valuation signals:** FCF yield is 6.45%, dividend yield is 2.00%, buyback yield is 3.65%, and shareholder yield is 5.65%. QCOM earns a lower-ranked spot because the quality is excellent, but the valuation margin is thinner than the top five.

## High-Quality Names Considered But Not Selected

- **Gilead Sciences (GILD):** Excellent reported quality metrics: ROE 43.36%, ROIC 27.70%, FCF margin 34.40%, Piotroski F-score 8, and $10.23 billion of trailing free cash flow. The reason it is not in the top six is valuation: 17.87x trailing earnings, 15.95x P/FCF, and 17.06x EV/FCF are reasonable but not clearly cheap after a 19.85% 52-week gain.
- **Citigroup (C):** Worth watching after restructuring progress and a strong bank earnings backdrop, but not selected because ROE is still only 7.65%, P/TBV is 1.42, operating cash flow is negative on the current screen, and bank earnings were a same-day event risk on July 14.
- **U.S. Bancorp (USB):** Better profitability than Citi, with ROE of 12.35% and a 3.34% dividend yield, but the valuation is not clearly better than CI, ACGL, or EG after a 32.24% 52-week move.
- **Danaher (DHR) and Thermo Fisher Scientific (TMO):** Durable franchises, but current valuation still looks too high for a strict value shortlist.
- **Global Payments (GPN):** Cheap-looking in some screens, but leverage, recent accounting noise, and integration/execution risk keep it below the quality-value threshold.

## Conclusion

The strongest current quality-value candidates are **Arch Capital Group (ACGL)**, **The Cigna Group (CI)**, and **Everest Group (EG)**. ACGL is the best all-around blend of low valuation, high returns, cash generation, and balance-sheet strength. CI is the best healthcare services cash-flow value. EG remains attractive near book value, but underwriting cyclicality and weaker revenue forecasts keep it behind ACGL and CI.

**Constellation Brands (STZ)** and **Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY)** are credible but riskier value ideas. **QUALCOMM (QCOM)** is a quality-first value candidate, suitable for continued monitoring where the valuation margin is not as wide but the business economics are stronger than most cheap stocks.
