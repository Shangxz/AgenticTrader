# Value Investing Weekly Report - 2026-W27

Updated through: 2026-07-01  
Scope: fresh quality-value review of US-listed public equities.  
Important note: this is research support, not personal financial advice. Valuation conclusions are probabilistic and may be wrong if fundamentals deteriorate or if the market is correctly discounting risks not yet visible in reported numbers.

## Short Market Context

US equities remain a selective market for value investors. StreetStats showed the S&P 500 at 7,440.43 as of the June 29 close, with a forward P/E of 20.04x and a trailing P/E of 24.67x. MarketWatch's first-half review also described a market up about 9.5% year to date, with AI hardware and semiconductor leadership offset by weakness in many software and online-services names. Schwab's mid-year outlook notes that 2026 S&P 500 earnings expectations have strengthened sharply, but that the improvement is concentrated and sits alongside inflation, policy, geopolitical, and long-rate risks.

That backdrop argues against buying low multiples mechanically. For the July 1 run, I used low valuation only as a starting filter, then favored companies with strong or defensible returns on capital, healthy free cash flow, reasonable debt, and a plausible reason why current market pricing may understate medium-term value. The ranking below was built fresh for today; earlier same-week picks were used only afterward to record changes.

## Today's Top Ideas - 2026-07-01

### 1. Arch Capital Group (ACGL)

**Why it looks undervalued:** ACGL trades near $97, with a market cap around $34 billion and a trailing P/E around 7.4x. StockAnalysis shows a forward P/E around 10.4x, price/free-cash-flow around 5.8x, price/book around 1.5x, EV/EBITDA around 6.0x, and EV/free-cash-flow around 6.1x. Those are still modest multiples for a specialty insurer with strong recent underwriting profitability.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** Arch's Q1 2026 results showed an 81.7% consolidated combined ratio and book value per common share of $66.19, up 1.7% from year-end 2025. StockAnalysis shows ROE around 21.3%, ROIC around 18.4%, debt/equity around 0.11, debt/FCF below 0.5x, and interest coverage above 38x. The quality case rests on underwriting discipline, conservative leverage, and long-term compounding of book value.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Insurance earnings are cyclical and can be lumpy. Catastrophe losses, adverse reserve development, pricing softness, or investment losses could make the current low P/E less meaningful. The thesis depends on underwriting discipline persisting through the cycle.

**Notable valuation signals:** ACGL remains the cleanest blend in today's review: low earnings and cash-flow multiples, high return metrics, low leverage, and a business model that has not been swept into the highest-multiple parts of the market.

### 2. Adobe (ADBE)

**Why it looks undervalued:** ADBE trades near $205, with a market cap around $82 billion and a trailing P/E around 11.7x. StockAnalysis shows a forward P/E around 7.9x, price/free-cash-flow around 7.9x, EV/EBITDA around 8.5x, and EV/free-cash-flow around 8.1x. Those are unusually low multiples for a scaled software franchise with high recurring revenue and very strong margins.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** Adobe's Q2 FY2026 release reported record revenue of $6.62 billion, up 13% year over year, or 11% in constant currency. StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 60.7%, ROE around 63.0%, interest coverage above 35x, free cash flow around $10.3 billion over the last 12 months, and a share count down about 5.8% year over year. The business still looks highly cash-generative even while the market worries about generative AI disruption.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** This is one of the highest-disruption ideas on the list. If AI-native creative or marketing tools permanently compress Adobe's pricing power, or if AI adoption fails to convert into durable paid demand, the low multiple may be justified. Recent investor concern around executive turnover also deserves monitoring.

**Notable valuation signals:** Adobe's combination of double-digit revenue growth, high ROIC, large free cash flow, and single-digit forward earnings/free-cash-flow multiples creates one of the more compelling quality-franchise discounts in the US market today.

### 3. Intuit (INTU)

**Why it looks undervalued:** INTU trades near $261 after a severe drawdown; StockAnalysis shows the stock down about 66% over the last 52 weeks. The current valuation is far below where this franchise historically traded: trailing P/E around 16.0x, forward P/E around 9.8x, price/free-cash-flow around 9.2x, EV/EBITDA around 11.2x, and EV/free-cash-flow around 9.2x.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** Intuit's fiscal Q3 2026 release showed total revenue of $8.6 billion, up 10%, with Consumer revenue up 8%, Global Business Solutions revenue up 15%, and Online Ecosystem revenue up 19%. The company raised full-year fiscal 2026 revenue and non-GAAP guidance, expected full-year revenue growth of about 13% to 14%, and repurchased $1.6 billion of stock in the quarter while authorizing a new $8 billion buyback. StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 20.6%, ROE around 22.5%, free cash flow around $7.8 billion, debt/FCF below 1x, and an Altman Z-Score above 7.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** The market's concern is not imaginary. AI-driven tax preparation and small-business finance tools could weaken TurboTax and QuickBooks pricing power, and Intuit's own fiscal Q3 details showed total TurboTax Online units expected to decline about 2% for the full year. The announced 17% workforce reduction also raises execution risk. This idea depends on Intuit using AI to deepen its platform rather than being displaced by it.

**Notable valuation signals:** INTU is added to the top-five list today because the valuation reset is now large enough to compensate for real disruption risk, while reported revenue growth, margins, free cash flow, and balance-sheet metrics still look like a quality business rather than a structurally broken one.

### 4. MarketAxess Holdings (MKTX)

**Why it looks undervalued:** MKTX trades near $113, with a market cap around $4.0 billion and a trailing P/E around 13.4x. StockAnalysis shows a forward P/E around 13.9x, price/free-cash-flow around 14.9x, EV/EBITDA around 8.8x, and EV/free-cash-flow around 14.2x. The stock is down roughly 48% over the last 52 weeks, leaving a formerly premium market-infrastructure franchise priced much more like a challenged cyclical.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** MarketAxess reported Q1 2026 record total revenue of $233.4 million, up 12%, including 12% growth in total commission revenue and 10% growth in services revenue. StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 32.4%, ROE around 24.3%, current ratio near 4.9, low debt, net cash, and interest coverage near 94x. The company still has attractive economics if its electronic bond-trading network remains competitively relevant.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** Competitive pressure is the central risk. If rivals, large dealers, or alternative protocols permanently reduce MarketAxess' take rates and liquidity advantage, historical return metrics may overstate future quality. The low Piotroski F-Score also keeps this below the cleaner compounders.

**Notable valuation signals:** MKTX ranks highly because the balance sheet is strong, returns remain high, and the current valuation already discounts a much tougher competitive backdrop.

### 5. Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY)

**Why it looks undervalued:** BMY trades near $58, with a market cap around $118 billion and a trailing P/E around 16.1x. StockAnalysis shows a forward P/E around 9.4x, price/free-cash-flow around 9.9x, EV/EBITDA around 8.1x, EV/free-cash-flow around 12.9x, and a dividend yield around 4.4%. The valuation remains inexpensive for a large pharmaceutical company if the Growth Portfolio can offset legacy-product erosion.

**Why it still qualifies as quality:** BMY's Q1 2026 revenue increased 3% to $11.5 billion, while Growth Portfolio revenue rose 12% to $6.2 billion. StockAnalysis shows trailing free cash flow around $11.9 billion, ROIC around 20.9%, ROE around 38.7%, and interest coverage around 8.5x. The quality case is not pristine, but the company has meaningful cash generation and a transition portfolio with observable growth.

**Main risks or disconfirming factors:** The patent-cliff risk remains real. Generic pressure, pipeline disappointments, acquisition debt, and integration risk could overwhelm the cash-flow case. BMY should be viewed as a discounted transition story rather than a simple high-quality compounder.

**Notable valuation signals:** A sub-10x forward P/E and roughly 10x price/free-cash-flow multiple give investors a tangible margin of safety if the Growth Portfolio continues to build enough scale.

## Watchlist, Not Top Ideas Today

### Qualcomm (QCOM)

QCOM remains a high-quality watchlist name, but it falls out of today's top five after a fresh relative ranking. StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 32.9%, debt/FCF around 1.2x, trailing free cash flow around $12.5 billion, a forward P/E around 18.8x, and price/free-cash-flow around 15.6x. Those quality metrics are strong, but the valuation is less clearly discounted than INTU, ADBE, ACGL, MKTX, or BMY. Handset concentration and the need to prove durable automotive, IoT, and AI-related diversification remain the main issues.

### Gilead Sciences (GILD)

GILD screens better than many large-cap pharmaceuticals on profitability and balance-sheet risk: StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 27.7%, free cash flow around $10.2 billion, debt/FCF around 2.2x, an Altman Z-Score around 4.5, and a Piotroski F-Score of 8. It did not make the top five because the valuation is less compelling after a positive 52-week move, with price/free-cash-flow around 15.3x and EV/free-cash-flow around 16.5x, and because 2026 earnings are complicated by large acquired R&D charges. It remains a credible quality watchlist name if the HIV franchise, oncology pipeline, and capital allocation continue to hold up.

### Paychex (PAYX)

PAYX is high quality, but not quite cheap enough for today's top five. Fiscal 2026 revenue reached $6.51 billion, up 17%, and adjusted EPS rose 11% to $5.51. StockAnalysis shows ROIC around 27.1%, operating margin around 39.5%, and free-cash-flow margin around 35.7%. The issue is valuation: the stock still trades around 20x trailing earnings and roughly 15x free cash flow, which is reasonable but not as asymmetric as the top ideas.

### T. Rowe Price (TROW)

TROW has a strong balance sheet, net cash, a dividend yield around 4.6%, and reasonable valuation metrics near 12x trailing earnings. The reason it remains a watchlist idea is business momentum: Q1 2026 AUM decreased to about $1.7 trillion and the firm continued to experience net outflows. It could become more compelling if flows stabilize, but for now its low multiple is partly explained by structural active-management pressure.

### Comcast (CMCSA)

CMCSA is statistically very cheap, with a trailing P/E below 5x and price/free-cash-flow near 4.3x, but it remains excluded from the top list because cheapness is heavily tied to structural concerns: broadband competition, video subscriber pressure, leverage, and low expected growth. The cash-flow yield is eye-catching, but the quality-value test requires clearer evidence that the market is over-discounting those pressures.

## Conclusion

The strongest current candidates are ACGL and ADBE. ACGL has the cleanest low-valuation, high-return, low-leverage profile. ADBE offers the largest quality-franchise discount if AI disruption fears prove overdone. INTU is the new addition: the valuation has compressed dramatically while revenue growth, cash generation, and balance-sheet quality still look strong, but it requires confidence that AI becomes a product tailwind rather than a moat-destroying substitute. MKTX remains compelling but depends on competitive stabilization in electronic credit trading. BMY is a cash-flow-supported pharmaceutical transition story with patent-cliff risk.
